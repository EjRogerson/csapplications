Predicting Wildfires with CAT Scans

Stanford engineers have used CAT scanners, typically used for examining the interior of a patient’s body in medicine, to study smouldering. This has allowed them to develop computer models capable of predicting where a wildfire may start next. The obvious benefits of this work include helping firefighters allocate their resources more efficiently, consequently reducing the loss of lives and properties due to wildfires.

Smouldering is the process of burning slowly where smoke is present without a flame, and the cause of consumption of more than half of plant matter which ends up burnt in wildfires [1]. Moreover, embers produced by smouldering logs can ignite fires in unsuspected areas when carried by hot updrafts, accelerating the spread of the fires, and have the potential to restart fires as smouldering can take place several days after flaming [2].

XCT (X-ray Computer Tomography) was used by Emeric Boigné to develop a new analytical method to study smouldering. Using this method provides a three-dimensional image of the material’s interior structure at a resolution of 135 μm [3, 4].

Through studying wood samples from around the country, the researchers aimed to compile a database of the different materials and their burn rates. Collecting this data then allowed Matt Bonanni (Stanford graduate) to use it alongside land contour data (as well as other relevant data such as the air humidity and wind patterns) to build a model for predicting fires. The simulation runs through many permuatations of different variables in order to predict how a fire is most likely to spread.

“Smoldering depends strongly on the heat exchange with the surrounding environment, ambient oxygen concentration, permeability, and density, as well as the content of moisture and inorganic material in the fuel" [4].

These simulations cover forest-level scales, therefore running them requires a great amount of processing power. For this reason, Tensor Flow, an open-source framework developed for use in machine learning, was adopted which vastly improved the performance of the model as many simulations were able to be completed simultaneously [3].

As the model was developed, simulations that took a day to complete before can now be run in close to real-time [3]. Improving the model to be able to predict the spread of fires in such a short time means that it can become fit for practical purposes where it can aid firefighters and influence their firefighting strategies.

The team of researchers is planning to continue the development of the model and refinement of their databases as well as exploring “zombie fires” using the newly developed technique [3].
